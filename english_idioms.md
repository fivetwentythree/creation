# English Idioms 

## What is the meaning of the idiom "to beat around the bush"?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206598 -->
To avoid talking about what is important; to delay getting to the point.

## In a conversation, if someone says "Stop beating around the bush," what do they want you to do?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206602 -->
They want you to say exactly what you mean directly and quickly.

## Rewrite this sentence using a native idiom: "He is avoiding the main topic because he is afraid of my reaction."

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206604 -->
"He is **beating around the bush** because he is afraid of my reaction."

## What is the origin/visual logic behind "beating around the bush"?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206605 -->
It comes from hunting; participants would hit the bushes to scare birds out for the hunter, rather than going directly into the dangerous thicket themselves.

## What is the meaning of the idiom "to bite the bullet"?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206608 -->
To force yourself to do something unpleasant or difficult that is necessary; to endure a painful situation.

## You have a dentist appointment you are dreading. How would you use "bite the bullet" in this context?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206609 -->
"I hate the dentist, but I just have to **bite the bullet** and go."

## How does "biting the bullet" differ from just "doing something"?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206611 -->
"Biting the bullet" implies the action is **unpleasant, painful, or dreaded**, but acknowledged as unavoidable.

## What does it mean to "cut corners"?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206613 -->
To do something in the easiest, cheapest, or fastest way, often resulting in poor quality or skipping rules.

## If a bridge collapses because the construction company "cut corners," what happened?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206615 -->
They likely used cheap materials, skipped safety inspections, or ignored regulations to save money or time.

## Why is "cutting corners" usually considered negative?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206617 -->
Because it sacrifices **quality or safety** for the sake of speed or cost.

## What does the idiom "hit the sack" (or "hit the hay") mean?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206619 -->
To go to bed in order to sleep.

## Replace the phrase in brackets with an idiom: "I'm exhausted, I think I'm going to [go to sleep] now."
"I'm exhausted, I think I'm going to **hit the sack** now."

## What is the meaning of "to play it by ear"?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206622 -->
To decide how to deal with a situation as it develops, rather than having a fixed plan.

## Your friend asks for a detailed itinerary for the weekend, but you prefer to be spontaneous. What idiom do you use?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206624 -->
"Let's not plan everything now; let's just **play it by ear**."

## What is the origin/analogy of "playing it by ear"?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206625 -->
It comes from music: playing a song by listening to the sounds rather than reading strict sheet music (a fixed plan).

## What is "the elephant in the room"?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206627 -->
A major problem or controversial issue that is obviously present but is being avoided or ignored by everyone.

## Why is it called "the elephant in the room"?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206629 -->
Because an elephant is huge and impossible to overlook, so ignoring it requires a deliberate effort to pretend it isn't there.

## In a meeting, everyone is discussing the budget, but no one mentions that the company is losing money. What is the company's loss?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206631 -->
It is **the elephant in the room**.

## What does it mean to do something "cold turkey"?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206633 -->
To stop a habit (usually an addiction like smoking or drinking) suddenly and completely, rather than gradually.

## What is the opposite of quitting "cold turkey"?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206635 -->
Tapering off, weaning off, or gradually reducing the habit.

## Complete the sentence: "He didn't use nicotine patches to quit smoking; he just quit _______."
**...cold turkey.**

## What does "to give someone the benefit of the doubt" mean?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206638 -->
To believe something good about someone, rather than something bad, when you have the possibility of doing either (usually due to lack of proof).

## Your friend is late. If you give them the "benefit of the doubt," what do you assume?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206640 -->
You assume they have a good reason (traffic, emergency) rather than a bad one (they forgot, they don't care).

## What does it mean to play "devil's advocate"?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206641 -->
To argue against a popular opinion or idea for the sake of debate or to test the strength of the opposing arguments.

## Why would someone play "devil's advocate" in a strategy meeting?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206643 -->
To uncover potential flaws in a plan by forcing the team to defend their ideas against criticism.

## Usage Check: Is a "devil's advocate" necessarily a person who disagrees with you?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206645 -->
No. They might actually agree with you, but they are arguing the opposite side just to test your logic.

## What does the idiom "under the weather" mean?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206646 -->
Feeling slightly sick, ill, or not your usual self (often used for colds, headaches, or hangovers).

## Why is "under the weather" a useful idiom for professional settings?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206648 -->
It allows you to say you are sick without giving specific, gross details about your illness.

## What does "to cut someone some slack" mean?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206649 -->
To treat someone less severely or critically than usual; to allow them some freedom or forgiveness.

## A new employee makes a mistake on their first day. Why should you "cut them some slack"?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206650 -->
Because they are new and still learning the ropes; strict criticism wouldn't be fair yet.

## What does it mean if something happens "once in a blue moon"?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206652 -->
It happens very rarely.

## How frequent is "once in a blue moon" compared to "occasionally"?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206653 -->
Much less frequent. "Occasionally" might be once a month; "**once in a blue moon**" might be once every few years.

## What does "to call it a day" mean?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206654 -->
To decide to stop working or doing an activity, usually because you are tired or have done enough.

## It is 6:00 PM and the team is tired. The manager says, "Let's call it a day." What happens next?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206656 -->
Everyone stops working and goes home.

## What is the difference between "giving up" and "calling it a day"?

<!-- notecardId: 1766651206658 -->
"Giving up" implies failure or quitting permanently. "**Calling it a day**" just means stopping for the present moment to rest.
